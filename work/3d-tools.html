SHAPEWAYS CREATOR & 3D TOOLS

From 2013-2015, the Creator team at Shapeways focused on one major problem: 3D printing products in over 50 materials, for designers all over the world, is a hard thing to do.

We made that easier by building software to help people better understand Shapeways' 3D printing materials, their production processes, and ultimately design better products.

The main interface for Shapeways' 3D Tools, iterated toward from 2013-2015 by the Creator team at Shapeways.

My Role

I was responsible for design and implementation of static HTML/CSS user interfaces for 3D Tools and supporting pages. The main 3D tools experience includes five different 3D visualizations built by Shapeways 3D Tools team, fully integrated material guidelines, and is built on a fluid grid system which enables designers on any setup to have a good view of things.

I worked together with full-stack application developers, 3D Tools engineers, product managers, and other designers during this project, and learned an insane amount of lessons along the way. More on the process later though (there's a lot there).

The problems

A significant amount of customer orders were failing due to designs being too thin to survive production. This wasn't the only reason, there were many, but research showed that it was a significant majority of rejections. These rejected orders resulted in frustrated designers, refunded credit cards, and a feeling of defeat for everyone involved.

In addition to thin walls causing rejections, our interfaces for uploading and managing model files needed to be more efficient, easy to use, and updated to integrate new features and experiences.

The goals

Drop the rejection rate of orders on Shapeways to less than 10% across the entire platform and all orders.

Improve the overall experience of ordering a 3D print from Shapeways by raising the quality of user interfaces, improving efficiency of navigation, and providing more valuable features.

One of countless UI iterations for model upload.

What we accomplished

Built, tested, iterated, and improved the main 3D tools experience from Fall of 2013 until Winter of 2015.

Integrated those same 3D tools into our internal system for our coworkers at the factories in NYC and Eindhoven, NL to use, which improved the consistency and efficiency of model checking and customer service.

Iterated on key interfaces for the platform: from model upload, to management, editing, and all the way through purchasing.

Integrated the new 3D Tools UI seamlessly into our ecosystem by updating and iterating on existing interfaces and flows.

My Models is where you manage your model files, add new ones, and easily order 3D prints.

The results

Rejected orders dropped well below our goal of 10%, and went from being our #1 net promoter score (NPS) detractor to not even in the top ten over the course of a year.

Each avoided rejection saved time, money, and headache for both the customer and Shapeways as a business. Reduced reprints contributed to improved gross margins, and factory operating efficiency.

The 3D Tools UI itself ended up becoming one of our highest performing "maker" page across several KPI's measured by the team.

At the end of the day, this effort was a massive success for both the business and the user experience of 3D printing with Shapeways.

I learned a lot due to the complexity of tools and systems that I was designing for, the responsibility of coding and styling the static frontend views, and working with a partially remote team.

Lessons learned

#1 - Good things take time. And that's okay. Delivering value to make people's lives is most important. It's hard to resist the appeal of one-off or snowflake solutions for problems that you can ship tomorrow. But if that decision weakens other parts of your experience, there has to be a better solution. Work for it. I find it critical to design systems that make sense end-to-end. No dead ends, no confusion, nobody left out to dry.

#2 - Welcome failure, but don't settle for it. Failing is the best way to learn, and should be accepted as part of the process. But it shouldn't be acceptable at certain point, either. Playtime and exploration are good times to fail, while shipping production code is not.

#3 - Validate or disprove your assumptions. The earlier the better. Validate on paper if you can. Validate with code prototypes if you need to, but don't let unchecked assumptions or guesses frame your decisionmaking when they're important ones to make.
