Shapeways is on a mission to help people create and customize all the products in their lives. In 2015, we built CustomMaker, which is a tool that enables automatic 3D customization of products. In 2016, we raised the quality of our shop owner tools by building product variants and improving our product edit flow and interface.

During that time, I've worked alongside product managers, frontend and application developers, 3D tools engineers, as well as community managers, marketers, and business owners. Together, we've greatly improved the quality of our shop owner tools with our work.

My responsibilities range from interface design to UX strategy, usability testing to prototyping and frontend implementation.

CustomMaker

During 2015, we built CustomMaker, which is a tool for programmatically adding custom text or images to a 3D printed product before you buy it. CustomMaker enables this customization to happen in real-time by manipulating the 3D geometry of the design based on shopper input.

I was responsible for designing the backend tools for designers to set this up on their products, and collaborated on the shopper-facing UI for actually customizing and buying the product. I also helped define product/UX strategy, collect and act on feedback/suggestions, and figure out how we'd ship this thing.

Designers set up customization "hotspots" where shoppers add text or images.

CustomMaker process

We started by doing our homework. Using market and consumer research compiled by our marketing team earlier in the year, we got to know the customers in our ecosystem that we were building for. We learned about their struggles with offering (and buying) customizable products with our current platform. We saw some painpoints to fix, and also recognized some opportunities to go above and beyond expectations.

Once we knew who we were building for, and why, the team defined the core use cases we wanted to support with our first launch. We did this for both shoppers looking to customize and buy a product, as well as the designers who were enabling this on their stuff. This was really intense, and was a massive learning experience in how to scope down almost never-ending possibility into a few month's worth of deliverable work.

After all that work of scoping and defining our use cases and goals, I got to work designing the experience. I researched a lot of 3D tools, studied their interfaces, and learned their interaction patterns. I spent a lot of time talking to and learning from our 3D tools team who are based on the West coast (those guys make me feel really dumb and I love it).

This research fueled and inspired my initial rounds of designs for the interfaces, their interactions, and how they would fit into our existing product. I got lots of feedback, iterated a bunch, and then we got a working prototype together.

With those rough prototypes in place, we started getting usability feedback by shipping to a small beta group of designers. With their stream of feedback to pick through, learn from, and act (or chose not to act) on, we rapidly got to a place people felt good about.

Product Variants

In Spring of 2016, we set out to build product variants, which is a system that allows for one product listing to have all the variations it has on the same page (e.g. a ring with sizes). Pretty standard e-commerce feature, but a big, complicated, and scary one to build from scratch (tech team did a data model redesign to support this project the first time we tried it in 2014).

On August 29th, the team shipped variants to the full user base after (non-consecutive) years of work, listening to users, iterating on the experience, and improving what we'd built.

I was responsible for designing the variants experience, the interfaces that powered the features, their interactions, and how it changed the overall system. Endless discussions, whiteboards, sketches, wireframes, and interactive prototypes were my deliverables.

Variants process

This year, when we set out to make variants happen once and for all (took a few tries), it started with whiteboard what-ifs, good ideas over beers after work, and sketches that got our brains excited with the possibilities. This scary, complex project was finally starting to feel conquerable. Doable. At this point, we had enough momentum to create wireframes that painted a better picture of what could be, which then turned into interactive prototypes that really sold the dream.

At that point it was time to get the prototype in front of some users, to validate some assumptions, test some guesses, and get a gut-check on the direction. We shared the prototype I made on our forums and to select community members, collected the feedback, and then got to work creating project milestones for implementation.

Lessons learned

#1 - As a team, define reachable project milestones. Code milestones and shippable milestones don't have to be the same thing. Allow time in between milestones for immediate feedback and iteration. There's an easy bias for moving on and shipping the next shiny thing, but it's important to iterate while things are fresh. It may change your original plans (for the better).

#2 - Lightweight, interactive prototypes go a really long way. They help immensely with selling the dream and getting buyin from stakeholders as well as users. Especially important with complex systems and interfaces, these tools are invaluable for having a living spec document where interactions can be experienced, flows can be tested, and functionality can be decided upon before trying to build the thing at scale.
