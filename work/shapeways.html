Trey McKay

2013 - 2016 SHAPEWAYS

From July 2013 to December 2016, I helped Shapeways solidify itself as the leading 3D printing service, marketplace, and community on the planet.

I designed and built for 3D tools, e-commerce tools, community forums, easy 3D creator apps, site-wide navigation improvements, and internal tools to name a few of the many things I worked on.

The gist of it

I pretty much ran the full gamut of experience design across the product. Strategy, research, flows and architecture, interactions, interfaces and visual design. I wrote a bunch of production HTML and SCSS for the frontend as well.

I worked alongside rad humans from technology, product, ux, community, marketing, sales, supply chain, and manufacturing to make it all happen.

This time at Shapeways has been a profound learning experience, and it wouldn't have been possible without the incredibly smart, talented, and driven individuals working there past and present. If you're one of those folks, thank you.

My focus

While my main area of expertise was Shapeways' maker and shop owner tools, I worked on projects that spanned more or less our entire software product. This included 3D visualizations and fixing tools, file upload and management experiences, easy 3D creator apps, and software-driven customization of 3D printed products.

In late 2015 and into 2016, I focused mainly on the backend of our marketplace. Improving the product listing experience, shop management tools, and quality of our information architecture and navigation patterns.

Good things happened

Due in part because of our work on 3D Tools, rejected orders (unavoidable failures in the factory) dropped from ~22% of all orders to below 5% within months of shipping 3D Tools in Spring 2015.

Each avoided rejection saved time, money, and headache for both the customer and Shapeways. Reduced re-prints of failed products improved gross margins and factory operating efficiency across the board as well. Rejections went from being Shapeways' #1 biggest customer complaint to not even being within the top ten.

On a personal level, I learned a lot due to the complexity of 3D tools and the service I was designing for, the responsibility of coding and styling the static frontend interfaces, and working with a partially remote team.

A few of many lessons

#1 - Learn from failure, but don't settle for it. When you're solving hard problems, the best solution rarely comes on the first try. Build process around this, build culture around this, and suddenly no problem is too hard.

#2 - Test assumptions. The earlier the better. Validate on paper early on. Validate with wireframes, clickable prototypes, or with code if you have to. But don't let unchecked assumptions or guesses frame your decisionmaking for too long, lest you're left with a shaky foundation.

#3 - The little details go a long way. And are usually a pain in the ass to get right. It's easy for teams to want to cut these first, but I'm a firm believer that loyalty and trust from your customers are hard to earn and easy to lose. As such, we have to get the little things right with what we make.
